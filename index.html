🔑 Key Trends Across Period 3

  • Atomic radius: DECREASES →   Increased nuclear charge, increased nuclear attraction, similar shielding
  • First ionisation energy: INCREASES →   Atomic radius decreases, nuclear charge and nuclear attraction increases, similar shielding
    ⚠️ Exceptions: Al (3p¹ electron easier to remove than 3s²) and S (electron pairing in 3p⁴)
  • Electronegativity: INCREASES →   Similar shielding, smaller atomic radius but increased nuclear charge and nuclear attraction

3. Properties of Period 3 Oxides

3.1 Structure and Melting Points

🔑 Melting Point Explanations

  • Ionic oxides (Na₂O, MgO, Al₂O₃): High m.p. due to strong electrostatic attractions between oppositely charged ions
  • MgO > Na₂O: Mg²⁺ has higher charge density than Na⁺
  • SiO₂: Macromolecular with strong covalent bonds throughout structure
  • P₄O₁₀, SO₂, SO₃: Simple molecular with weak Van der Waals forces
  • P₄O₁₀ > SO₃ > SO₂: Larger molecules = stronger VdW forces
Experimental Test — Distinguishing Ionic from Covalent Oxides by Electrical Conductivity

AQA may ask you to describe an experiment to distinguish between ionic and covalent oxides. The key test is electrical conductivity of the molten substance:
  • Ionic oxides (e.g. Na₂O, MgO, Al₂O₃): Do not conduct electricity when solid (ions are fixed in the lattice), but do conduct when molten because the ions become free to move and carry charge.
  • Giant covalent oxides (e.g. SiO₂): Do not conduct electricity in either the solid or molten state, because there are no ions or delocalised electrons — only covalent bonds between atoms.
  • Simple molecular oxides (e.g. P₄O₁₀, SO₂): Do not conduct in any state — no charged particles.
Method: Heat the solid oxide until it melts, then place two electrodes into the liquid connected to a bulb or ammeter in a circuit. If the bulb lights / ammeter deflects, the substance is ionic. If not, it is covalent.

Tip: You can also test aqueous solutions — dissolved ionic compounds conduct, but this only works for soluble oxides.

3.2 Reactions of Oxides with Water

Basic Oxides (Metal Oxides)

Na₂O(s) + H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq)

pH 12–14

MgO(s) + H₂O(l) → Mg(OH)₂(aq)

pH 9–10 (sparingly soluble)

Al₂O₃: INSOLUBLE in water (amphoteric)

Why Al₂O₃ is insoluble in water (AQA key point): Al₂O₃ has a giant ionic lattice in which the Al³⁺ and O²⁻ ions are held together by very strong electrostatic forces of attraction. The oxide ions (O²⁻) are held too strongly in the lattice for water molecules to break it apart. The high charge densities of both Al³⁺ (3+, small ion) and O²⁻ mean the lattice energy is very high, so the energy released by hydration of the ions is insufficient to overcome the lattice enthalpy.

Acidic Oxides (Non-metal Oxides)

SiO₂: INSOLUBLE in water (but still acidic — reacts with bases)

Why SiO₂ is insoluble in water (AQA key point): SiO₂ has a giant covalent (macromolecular) structure. Each silicon atom is covalently bonded to four oxygen atoms in a tetrahedral arrangement, forming a continuous 3D network. The strong covalent bonds (Si–O) throughout the structure are too strong for water molecules to break. Unlike simple molecular oxides such as SO₂ or P₄O₁₀, there are no discrete molecules that can interact with and dissolve in water. However, SiO₂ is still classified as acidic because it does react with hot, concentrated alkali:
SiO₂(s) + 2NaOH(aq) → Na₂SiO₃(aq) + H₂O(l)
P₄O₁₀(s) + 6H₂O(l) → 4H₃PO₄(aq)

pH 0–2 (phosphoric acid)

SO₂(g) + H₂O(l) ⇌ H₂SO₃(aq)

pH 2–3 (sulfurous acid)

SO₂ / H₂SO₃ — Weak Acid Equilibrium (AQA key point):
The reversible arrow (⇌) in the equation above is critical. H₂SO₃ is a weak acid — it only partially dissociates in water: H₂SO₃(aq) ⇌ H⁺(aq) + HSO₃⁻(aq) Because the equilibrium lies to the left, only a small proportion of H₂SO₃ molecules donate a proton at any time. This is why the pH of the solution is around 2–3 rather than 0.

Contrast with SO₃ / H₂SO₄: When SO₃ dissolves in water the reaction goes to completion (forward arrow only →), producing sulfuric acid, H₂SO₄. Sulfuric acid is a strong acid — it fully dissociates: H₂SO₄(aq) → 2H⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq) This means all H₂SO₄ molecules donate both protons, giving a much lower pH of 0–1.

Exam tip: If asked to explain the difference in pH between solutions of SO₂ and SO₃ in water, the answer centres on weak vs strong acid behaviour and partial vs complete dissociation.
SO₃(g) + H₂O(l) → H₂SO₄(aq)

pH 0–1 (sulfuric acid — strong acid, fully dissociates)

3.3 Reactions with Acids and Bases

Basic Oxides + Acids → Salt + Water

Na₂O + H₂SO₄ → Na₂SO₄ + H₂O MgO + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂O

Acidic Oxides + Bases → Salt + Water

SiO₂(s) + 2NaOH(aq) → Na₂SiO₃(aq) + H₂O(l) P₄O₁₀(s) + 12NaOH(aq) → 4Na₃PO₄(aq) + 6H₂O(l) SO₂(g) + NaOH(aq) → NaHSO₃(aq) SO₃(g) + 2NaOH(aq) → Na₂SO₄(aq) + H₂O(l)

Aluminium Oxide — AMPHOTERIC (reacts with both acids AND bases)

🔑 Definition — Amphoteric: A species that can act as both an acid and a base

⚠️ Exam Alert: Al₂O₃ is AMPHOTERIC — know equations for both acid and base reactions!

Acting as a BASE (reacting with acids):

Al₂O₃(s) + 6HCl(aq) → 2AlCl₃(aq) + 3H₂O(l) Al₂O₃(s) + 3H₂SO₄(aq) → Al₂(SO₄)₃(aq) + 3H₂O(l)

Acting as an ACID (reacting with bases):

Al₂O₃(s) + 2NaOH(aq) + 3H₂O(l) → 2NaAl(OH)₄(aq)

Forms sodium aluminate

4. Aluminium Chemistry — Special Cases

4.1 The [Al(H₂O)₆]³⁺ Ion (Hexaaqua Aluminium Ion)

🔑 Formation: When aluminium salts dissolve in water, Al³⁺ ions are surrounded by 6 water molecules via coordinate bonds.

  • High charge density of Al³⁺ (small, highly charged) polarises O–H bonds in water molecules
  • This weakens O–H bonds, making it easier for H⁺ to be released
  • Makes aluminium salt solutions ACIDIC (pH ≈ 3)
[Al(H₂O)₆]³⁺(aq) + H₂O(l) ⇌ [Al(H₂O)₅(OH)]²⁺(aq) + H₃O⁺(aq)

Hydrolysis reaction — produces H₃O⁺ making solution acidic

📝 Always draw the hexaaqua ion with square brackets and 3+ charge outside: [Al(H₂O)₆]³⁺

4.2 Reactions of [Al(H₂O)₆]³⁺ with Bases

A) With LIMITED OH⁻ (e.g. adding NaOH dropwise)

[Al(H₂O)₆]³⁺(aq) + 3OH⁻(aq) → Al(OH)₃(s) + 6H₂O(l)

White precipitate forms

B) With EXCESS OH⁻ (e.g. excess NaOH)

The precipitate redissolves:

Al(OH)₃(s) + OH⁻(aq) → [Al(OH)₄]⁻(aq)

Colourless solution forms (aluminate ion)

⚠️ Common Exam Question: Describe what you would observe when NaOH is added dropwise to an aluminium salt solution until in excess:
1. Initially: White precipitate forms
2. With excess NaOH: Precipitate dissolves to give a colourless solution

C) With NH₃ (Ammonia Solution)

[Al(H₂O)₆]³⁺(aq) + 3NH₃(aq) → Al(OH)₃(s) + 3NH₄⁺(aq) + 3H₂O(l)

White precipitate forms but does NOT dissolve in excess NH₃

📝 Key Difference: Al(OH)₃ dissolves in excess NaOH but NOT in excess NH₃ (NH₃ is not a strong enough base)

4.3 Complete Summary of Al(OH)₃ Amphoteric Behaviour

🔑 Al(OH)₃ is AMPHOTERIC — reacts with both acids and bases!

Acting as a BASE (with acids):

Al(OH)₃(s) + 3H⁺(aq) → [Al(H₂O)₆]³⁺(aq)

OR simplified: Al(OH)₃(s) + 3H⁺(aq) → Al³⁺(aq) + 3H₂O(l)

Acting as an ACID (with bases):

Al(OH)₃(s) + OH⁻(aq) → [Al(OH)₄]⁻(aq)

White precipitate dissolves in excess alkali

📝 Final Exam Tips

  • Always balance equations and include state symbols
  • Be specific about bonding and structure when explaining properties
  • Remember exceptions: Al (amphoteric), Si/P/S (don't react with water)
  • For melting points, mention the TYPE of force being overcome
  • Don't confuse "atoms", "ions", and "molecules" — use the correct term!
  • Practice drawing dot-and-cross diagrams for complex ions like [Al(H₂O)₆]³⁺
  • For insolubility of Al₂O₃: lattice too strong for water to break apart. For SiO₂: covalent bonds too strong for water to break.
  • SO₂ in water → weak acid (partial dissociation, pH 2–3). SO₃ in water → strong acid (full dissociation, pH 0–1).
  • To distinguish ionic from covalent oxides experimentally: melt and test electrical conductivity.

⚡ Quick Memory Tricks

  • Metal oxides = BASIC (except Al₂O₃ which is special)
  • Non-metal oxides = ACIDIC (except SiO₂ which is insoluble)
  • Giant structures = HIGH melting points
  • Simple molecular = LOW melting points
  • "NASA" = Na, Al, Si, Ar don't fit the normal I.E. trend perfectly
  • Amphoteric = "Amph" (both) + "teric" (character)
  • SO₂ = weak acid, SO₃ = strong acid — the extra oxygen makes all the difference!
  • Melt it, test it — ionic oxides conduct when molten, covalent ones don't
